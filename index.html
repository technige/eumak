Wait, what? Why?

My life exists across multiple languages: my family is British/German and my employer is Swedish. Therefore on a typical day, I find myself typing words like Schultüte or Malmö and having to time the compose key incantation precisely so I don't end up working in Malm"o.

I've spoken to friends, more multilingually gifted than I, who understandably bemoan the need to switch keyboard layouts several times a day as they switch languages, depending on who they're typing to.

And I'm also a bit of a geek, so I thought I'd see how difficult it would be to come up with a reasonably natural keyboard layout that catered for as many European languages as possible. Well, actually just the Latin-based ones... but I reasoned that should still be enough of a challenge!

The basic alphabet

The layout is grounded in a QWERTY format. While other formats (AZERTY, QWERTZ, etc) are commonly in use by some language-specific layouts, QWERTY is both the original and the most common configuration. There is no perfect choice here, but QWERTY should provide the greatest possible degree of familiarity to the most people.

Extra vowels

The five keys to the east of P and L are typically occupied by a mixture of alphabetic and punctuation symbols. Eumak assigns these as extra vowels, since vowels undergo a high degree of modification across European languages.

More specifically, these five keys are assigned to the diaeresis/umlaut variants of the basic latin vowels, i.e. ä ë ï ö ü. Not only are these very common across European languages, but they include the only already-accented characters used as a base for further modification. These are ö and ü which Hungarian transforms with an acute accent to ő and ű.

The positions of Ä, Ö and Ü are taken from German, Swedish and Finnish. Ë and Ï are more arbitrarily laid out, since only Albanian keyboards have a key for Ë, despite the character also being used by French, Dutch and Welsh orthographies.

New modifiers

By repurposing a couple of keys, the layout provides a two extra modifiers alongside the regular ones you'll find on most keyboards. Specifically, these are the Mod and AltMod keys which, unsurprisingly, stand for "Modifier" and "Alternate Modifier" respectively.

Mod is located to the left of Z, replacing a key typically used for punctuation. AltMod is located to the right of AltGr, replacing the right Super/Windows key.

These modifers work identically to the existing Shift or AltGr keys inasmuch as they require holding down while the key requiring modification is pressed. For keyboards which don't provide a right Super/Windows key (and hence no home for AltMod) the combination of AltGr+Mod is equivalent.

Broadly speaking, the Mod key adds an acute accent and the AltMod key adds a circumflex (for vowels) or a caron (for consonants). Vowels here include not only a e i o u but also w and y, which can be accented in Welsh. E is considered a special case, since it can take either a circumflex (in French, Portuguese, Slovene, Welsh and Breton) or a caron (in Czech). To work around this, ě can be typed using AltGr+J.

What is included?

The keyboard attempts to provide full support for the following language groups:

West Germanic (English, German & Dutch)

Romance (French, Italian, Spanish, Romanian, Portuguese & Catalan)

West Slavic (Polish, Czech & Slovak)

South Slavic (Serbian, Croatian, Bosnian, Slovene & Montenegrin)

North Germanic (Swedish, Danish, Norweigian, Icelandic & Faroese)

Finno-Ugric (Hungarian, Finnish & Estonian)

Albanian

Celtic (Irish, Welsh, Breton & Scottish Gaelic)

What isn't included?

  • Greek (with the exception of µ, which is included due to its presence in ISO 8859-1 and as an SI prefix).
  • Cyrillic
  • Maltese
  • Baltic languages (Latvian, Latgalian and Lithuanian), which require an extra 12 unique characters not found in other language groups (ā ē ė ģ į ī ķ ļ ņ ō ų ū) therefore giving a regrettably high cost of inclusion.
