Wait, what? Why?

My life exists across multiple languages: my family is British/German and my employer is Swedish. Therefore on a typical day, I find myself typing words like Schultüte or Malmö and having to time the compose key incantation precisely so I don't end up working in Malm"o.

I've spoken to multilingual friends who bemoan the need to switch keyboard layouts several times a day as they switch languages, depending on who they're typing to.

On top of that, I'm also a bit of a geek, so I thought I'd see how difficult it would be to come up with a reasonably natural keyboard layout that catered for as many European languages as possible. And It turned out that with a bit of thought, I could make all the official EU languages available through one keyboard.

Even Greek. Sort of.

Layout overview

The layout is grounded in QWERTY. While other formats (AZERTY, QWERTZ, etc) are commonly in use by some language-specific layouts, QWERTY is both the original and the most common configuration. There is no perfect choice here, but QWERTY arguably provides the greatest degree of familiarity to the most people.

To the east of P and L are located five extra vowels. These keys are typically occupied by a mixture of alphabetic and punctuation symbols, but are repurposed by Eumak as ä ë ï ö ü. Not only are these vowel variants very common across European languages, but they include the only already-accented characters used as a base for further modification: ö and ü which Hungarian transforms with an acute accent to ő and ű.

Numbers hold their regular place on the top row with shifted values filled by the characters most commonly found in those positions. And six symbol keys fill most of the remaining spots, with one to the left of the numbers, two to the right, and a further three east of M.

The final key, to the left of Z, is Mod (the modifier), which can be used to access accented characters. This key is usually assigned to symbols (most commonly greater than and less than signs) but can be thought of in Eumak as a new type of Shift.

Letters

The basic Latin latters are available from the same places they are usually found on any QWERTY keyboard. And as expected, lower case is typed by default, upper case is typed with Shift.

Vowels with umlauts/diaereses/tremas can be entered in the same way as the basic letters by using the appropriately marked keys. The semi-vowels ẅ and ÿ, used in Dutch, French and Welsh, can be typed via AltGr+Ë and AltGr+Ï respectively.

What is included?

The keyboard attempts to provide full support for the following language groups:

West Germanic (English, German & Dutch)

Romance (French, Italian, Spanish, Romanian, Portuguese & Catalan)

West Slavic (Polish, Czech & Slovak)

South Slavic (Serbian, Croatian, Bosnian, Slovene & Montenegrin)

North Germanic (Swedish, Danish, Norweigian, Icelandic & Faroese)

Finno-Ugric (Hungarian, Finnish & Estonian)

Albanian

Celtic (Irish, Welsh, Breton & Scottish Gaelic)

What isn't included?

  • Greek (with the exception of µ, which is included due to its presence in ISO 8859-1 and as an SI prefix).
  • Cyrillic
  • Maltese
  • Baltic languages (Latvian, Latgalian and Lithuanian), which require an extra 12 unique characters not found in other language groups (ā ē ė ģ į ī ķ ļ ņ ō ų ū) therefore giving a regrettably high cost of inclusion.
